01  —  the strangeness of it

Philosophy
is not a
clinical skill.

And yet.

Here we are.

The NHS does not traditionally hire Applied Philosophers. It hires clinicians, administrators, analysts, project managers. People with defined roles and defined outputs. The system is built for legibility — for people who can be placed on an org chart without ambiguity.

An Applied Philosopher resists that. The discipline is concerned with one thing: how do people actually think, decide, behave and suffer — and what can be built, designed or organised to serve that better? It is not abstract. It is radically practical. It just operates at a level of abstraction that most institutions don't have the patience for.

The NHS, at this particular moment, needs exactly that patience. Because what it is attempting — reorganising itself around the human being in the place where they live — is a design problem before it is a logistics one. And someone is working on it from the inside.

02  —  the moment

Two documents landed in quick succession. Together they represent the most significant reorientation of the NHS in a generation. The neighbourhood is now the unit.

July 2025

NHS 10 Year Plan

Care moves from hospital to community. From reactive to preventive. From institution to place. The plan names the neighbourhood as the primary site of health — not the ward, not the referral pathway. The street. The home. The life being lived.

March 2026

NHS Neighbourhood Health Framework

Operational shape to that ambition. How teams form across boundaries that previously didn't speak. How data flows without losing the person it belongs to. How technology serves the human in the place — rather than the system's need to account for them.

This is a design problem at civilisational scale. Someone working inside it — who thinks philosophically, builds practically, and understands both the policy and the person — is sitting at a junction that very few people occupy. Google DeepMind is working on health. Google Creative Lab is working on what technology means to people. That junction exists in Dorset right now.

Digital and Transformation Lead. Five GP practices. 74,000 patients on the Dorset coast. Building the neighbourhood health infrastructure the NHS now says it needs.

He holds a BA in Applied Philosophy and Political Studies and a Postgraduate Diploma in Psychology. He describes himself as an Opsimath — someone who arrives at knowledge late, by an unusual route, and finds that the detour was the point.

He co-founded Neighbourhood CoLab CIC with three colleagues: an NHS nurse, a clinical psychologist, and a social worker. Between the four of them, over a century of frontline experience. The CIC is how he works with the outside world.

He invented a format called the Scrollsite — a bespoke single-page artefact built for one person before one conversation. A mirror, pointed carefully at the reader. He has built over 250 of them. You are reading one now.
